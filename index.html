Non-linear impacts of bed nets

1 The Ross–Macdonald equation

Ronald Ross and George Macdonald's simple model of R₀, the basic reproduction number: the average number of new human infections caused by one infected person dropped into a fully susceptible population.

If R₀ > 1, malaria spreads. If R₀ < 1, it will be eliminated. The model:

R0 = m a2 b c r  ×  pn −ln p
  • mmosquitoes per person
  • abites per mosquito per day
  • b,ctransmission efficiency (each way)
  • rhuman recovery rate
  • pmosquito daily survival
  • ndays the parasite needs to mature

The two highlighted parts are exactly what bed nets act on. The biting rate a enters squared, while the survival terms are exponential and it's that exponential piece that makes the ITN impact non-linear. The rest of this page zooms in on them.

2 The non-linearity

When a mosquito bites an infected person, it doesn't become infectious straight away. The parasite needs to develop inside it for about n ≈ 10 days: the extrinsic incubation period. Only a mosquito that survives those 10 days can transmit.

Mosquito lifespans follow an exponential distribution: each day it survives with probability p, so the chance of still being alive after n days is pn. Because that's a power, a small nudge to daily survival gets amplified enormously over 10 days.

The curve shows the fraction of mosquitoes still alive each day. The shaded slice past day 10 is the only part that can ever transmit malaria and reducing daily survival even a little reduces that tail fast.

Drop daily survival from 0.90 to 0.80 and survival to day 10 falls from about 35% to 11%. That's roughly a three-fold reduction, demonstrating the non-linearity.

3 The impact of bed nets

Insecticide-treated nets (ITNs) impact the equation in two places at once: they physically stop bites (lowering a, which enters squared) and their insecticide kills mosquitoes that land on them (lowering daily survival p, which feeds the pn term).

Usage is the proportion of people sleeping under a net, so it determines how often a host-seeking mosquito encounters a net. At low coverage most mosquitoes find an unprotected person and feed freely; as coverage climbs, a growing fraction of feeding attempts are turned away at the net (pulling down the average biting rate a) and a growing fraction end with the mosquito picking up a lethal dose of insecticide (pulling down daily survival p).
