A worked example

Non-linearities in malaria epidemiology and control

From coverage to clinical cases

bed-net coverage→ EIR →prevalence (PfPR) →clinical incidence

PfPR is the outcome we can measure and map at scale, so clinical incidence is usually read off it through an empirical PfPR→incidence relationship (a conversion, not a causal step). Each link below is treated in turn, with evidence and mechanism, followed by an interactive panel that traces a combined bed-net effect (barrier plus insecticidal killing) through the cascade.

1 Bed-net coverage and vector control

A continuous community effect, not a hard threshold

Evidence

Insecticide-treated nets protect users directly, and protect users and non-users indirectly by lowering mosquito survival and density and shrinking the infectious reservoir. Non-users benefit when nearby households use nets. This community effect is not a herd-immunity threshold: it appears even at low usage and grows continuously as coverage rises.

Direct protection scales with personal use; the indirect effect scales with community coverage and depends on vector behaviour, insecticide efficacy, resistance, net durability and spatial clustering. Pyrethroid resistance erodes the insecticidal benefits but leaves the physical barrier intact.

Mechanism

The Ross–Macdonald expression for vectorial capacity makes the non-linearity explicit:

C = m a2 pn −log p

where m is mosquitoes per person, a the biting rate, p daily mosquito survival and n the extrinsic incubation period. A treated net both reduces bites and kills mosquitoes, shortening survival so that fewer live long enough to complete the incubation period n.

Biting enters squared (a2) and survival as pn, so a modest change in either produces a much larger change in transmission. Baseline transmission also spans a wide range: estimated R₀ runs from about 1 to over 3,000 across African settings (Smith et al.).

2 From EIR to prevalence (PfPR)

A steeply saturating curve

Evidence

The relationship between the entomological inoculation rate (EIR) and parasite prevalence is strongly non-linear. At low EIR, small changes produce large changes in prevalence; at high EIR, PfPR saturates, so large differences in EIR map to small differences in prevalence. Cross-site analyses find PfPR tracks roughly the logarithm of annual EIR: some sites with annual EIR below 5 still exceed 40% PfPR, while high-EIR sites cluster near a ceiling.

Two features sustain this. Biting is heterogeneous (a minority of people receive most infectious bites), and infections last around six months, so prevalence integrates exposure over time rather than tracking it instantly.

Mechanism

A minimal prevalence model captures the shape:

PfPR ≈ λ λ + r

where λ is the force of infection (related to EIR) and r the clearance rate. At low λ prevalence rises sharply; at high λ most people are already infected, so it approaches a ceiling. In practice the ceiling is reinforced by superinfection (a further bite does not change a positive survey), long infection duration, heterogeneous biting, and immunity effects on parasite density and detectability.

3 From prevalence to clinical incidence

Shaped by immunity and exposure history

Evidence

We use the fitted Cameron et al. (2015) relationship between PfPR and clinical incidence, and plot the matched field data it draws on (Battle et al. 2015) alongside it. Incidence data are sparser and less standardised than prevalence surveys, and the relationship depends on age and immunity rather than being a single universal curve.

At low transmission, incidence scales roughly linearly with prevalence. As transmission rises, prevalence saturates while clinical episodes continue through repeated infection and superinfection, producing convexity, most strongly in young children. The age pattern shifts with it: below about 10% PfPR much of the burden falls on adults; at higher transmission it concentrates in children. After a recent decline in transmission, young children carry a larger share, because older groups retain immunity acquired under previously higher transmission.

Mechanism

The driver is exposure-acquired immunity. Clinical incidence decomposes as:

clinical incidence = infection incidence × P(infection → disease)

PfPR reflects the proportion infected; clinical incidence depends on how often people are infected and how often infection becomes febrile. The second term falls with accumulated exposure and is age- and history-dependent: a setting that has long sat at 30% PfPR differs from one that has just fallen from 70%. PfPR is an equilibrium marker of infection; clinical incidence is a dynamic outcome shaped by recent exposure.

What to notice

Each step is non-linear in a different way, and they compound. Plot 1 falls steeply then flattens (biting squared, survival exponential). Plot 2 saturates, so the same EIR reduction buys less prevalence change when baseline transmission is high. Plot 3's slope depends on age (convex in young children, near-flat in adults), so a prevalence fall reduces young-child cases substantially but adult cases little.

Baseline transmission sets the response. At high baseline EIR the operating point sits on the PfPR plateau, so a large transmission reduction moves prevalence, and clinical incidence, only slightly. At low baseline EIR the same coverage produces a much larger prevalence and case response. Returns therefore increase as transmission falls.

A small prevalence change is not the same as no effect. At high transmission a real reduction in biting can be hidden within a saturated prevalence figure while still lowering clinical disease. The conclusion depends on which outcome is measured.

The size of the effect. The net's effects on biting and survival are taken from experimental-hut data, so the impact on clinical incidence is not tuned: it falls out at the end of the cascade. At high transmission and ~80% coverage the modelled reduction in under-5 clinical incidence is about 50%, close to the ~50% fewer uncomplicated episodes reported by the Cochrane review of ITN trials (Lengeler 2004). This is a best case: a new net with no insecticide resistance.

Methods. An illustrative model showing the shape of these relationships, not a specific setting. Not to be used for decision making. The no-net baseline EIR is set by the slider and coverage is capped at 80%.

  • Coverage → EIR. The net's effect on the vector is taken from experimental-hut quantities for a generic new pyrethroid net with no insecticide resistance: a blood-feeding inhibition β = 0.70 reduces biting to a0(1−βC), and a per-encounter mortality μ = 0.45 reduces daily survival to p0(1−μC)1/g via the gonotrophic cycle (g = 3 days; Le Menach 2007, Griffin et al. 2010). These feed the Ross–Macdonald a2·pn term. The resulting EIR reduction (~98% at 80% coverage) is an output, comparable to the ~87% in Unwin et al. (2023), which reflects operational rather than new-net conditions. This step is more mechanistic than the two data-fitted steps below.
  • EIR → PfPR. A saturating fit to P. falciparum PfPR in children <15 against annual EIR across African sites (Hay et al. 2005).
  • PfPR → incidence. The fitted Cameron et al. (2015) relationship (Griffin individual-based model, low seasonality), digitised in absolute units (cases·person⁻¹·yr⁻¹) for three age groups. The dots are 279 matched incidence and prevalence records for P. falciparum in sub-Saharan Africa (Battle et al. 2015; Cameron calibration subset), one point per record, coloured by reported age group (young ≤6, older 4–16, adult ≥15).
  • Summary readout. The reduction in under-5 (0–5y) clinical incidence, matching the age range of the Cochrane trial measure.
  • Age band. The EIR–PfPR data is PfPR in children <15 (Hay et al. 2005); Cameron's relationship uses PfPR2–10. Both are childhood references, so the cascade runs on roughly one scale; PfPR<15 sits slightly below PfPR2–10, but the offset is small and largely cancels in the relative reduction. The cross-site incidence scatter is dominated by study design (detection method, case definition).
