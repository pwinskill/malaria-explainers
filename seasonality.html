Seasonality and the timing of malaria control

Where transmission is seasonal, most of the year's malaria falls in a few months. A preventive treatment given to children protects them only for a few weeks, so a seasonal programme has to time its rounds to the season. This explainer looks at how much depends on when those rounds land, and on who they reach.1

1 Malaria cases are concentrated in a season

Evidence

In much of the Sahel and other highly seasonal settings, rainfall is confined to a short wet season. Mosquito numbers, transmission and clinical cases rise and fall with it, so a large share of the year's disease in young children arrives in a window of two to four months.2 The more concentrated the season, the more of the annual burden sits inside that window.

Mechanism

Rainfall fills the breeding sites that mosquito larvae develop in, so the mosquito population, and with it the rate of infectious bites, tracks the rains. Because the aquatic stages take time to develop and the adult population builds up gradually, the peak in transmission, and then in cases, lags the peak in rainfall by weeks, with the exact lag depending on the setting.3 A concentrated rainy season therefore produces a single, delayed peak in childhood cases rather than a flat year-round level.

2 Preventive treatment protects for only a few weeks

Protection from one dose against days since it was given, using the SP-AQ prophylaxis curve from malariasimulation. It stays near full for about two weeks, then falls steeply, dropping from half to a tenth in little more than a week.

Evidence

Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) gives children aged 3 to 59 months a full antimalarial course at monthly intervals during the transmission season.1 Each course clears any current infection and then protects against new infection while the drug persists, but that protection is temporary: the drug is eliminated over the following weeks and protection wanes with it.

Mechanism

In the model, protection from a dose decays following a Weibull survival curve, e(t) = exp(−(t/λ)k), where t is days since the dose.4 For the sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine plus amodiaquine (SP-AQ) combination used in SMC, the fitted shape k = 4.3 and scale λ = 38.1 days give near-complete protection for about two weeks, then a steep fall: half at roughly 35 days and a tenth by around 46 days. This plateau then cliff is why the rounds are spaced about a month apart. SP-AQ is the standard SMC regimen; where resistance to it is high the protective duration is shorter, and alternative drugs are under study.

3 Covering the season is a timing problem

Evidence

Because each dose protects for only a few weeks, a single round cannot cover a season that lasts several months. SMC is delivered as a block of monthly rounds, typically four or five, timed to span the period of highest transmission.2 How much disease the programme prevents depends on how well that block of protection lines up with the peak in cases.

Mechanism

Total protection through the year is the envelope of the individual dose curves, one per round. The number of rounds and the spacing between them set how much of the season is covered:

  • Too few rounds leave the shoulders of the season uncovered, so cases before the first round or after the last are not prevented.
  • Spacing wider than the drug lasts opens gaps between rounds, where protection has waned before the next dose restores it, and cases slip through.
  • Rounds aligned with the peak put the highest protection where the most cases are, which is where each dose averts the most disease.

Because cases are concentrated near the peak, moving the same rounds a few weeks earlier or later changes how much disease they prevent by much more than the change in timing might suggest.

4 When the season shifts, a fixed calendar misses it

Evidence

SMC rounds are scheduled to a calendar set in advance, usually from the historical timing of the rains. When the season arrives earlier or later than that calendar assumes, the same rounds line up poorly with the peak and prevent fewer cases. The onset and length of rainy seasons already vary from year to year, and climate change is projected to shift the timing, length and geography of transmission seasons,5 so a calendar fixed to a long-run average can drift out of step with the season that actually occurs.

Mechanism

Shifting the season moves the case peak along the year while the scheduled rounds stay put. The block of protection then straddles the shoulder of the season rather than its peak, so it overlaps fewer cases and averts less. The effect is strongest in the most concentrated seasons, where the peak is narrow and a modest shift can move most of the cases outside the covered window. Timing the rounds to the observed case peak, rather than to the first rains, keeps them aligned.3

5 Reaching the same children, or different ones

Evidence

No round reaches every eligible child. Coverage is the share reached in a round, but two programmes with the same coverage can protect very differently depending on who is reached across rounds. If the same children are reached each time, a fixed group is well covered while the rest are never reached. If the children reached vary between rounds, more children receive at least one course, but fewer are covered through the whole season.6 Whether the same or different children are reached across rounds, the correlation between rounds, is a parameter in transmission models, not just a detail of delivery.4

Mechanism

At a fixed coverage per round, the correlation between rounds sets how doses are shared out:

  • Same children. A fixed covered group receives every round and is well protected, but the uncovered share, one minus coverage, receives nothing all season and keeps contributing cases. Extra rounds fall on children who are often already protected, so doses are used less efficiently.
  • Random. Each round reaches an independent draw, so a child protected in one round may be missed in the next. Over several rounds most children receive at least one course, which shrinks the never-covered group, though coverage in any single round is unchanged.
  • Different children. An idealised extreme, shown to mark the far end of the trade-off: rounds are deliberately spread across children so the largest number receive at least one course. This reaches the most children, but each is covered for a smaller part of the season, so continuous protection through the peak is weaker.

Because a second dose given while the first is still protecting adds little, spreading doses across more children usually averts more cases than concentrating them on the same children. The exception is when rounds are spaced so widely that even the same children need each round to stay protected.

In practice coverage is positively correlated between rounds: the same hard-to-reach children tend to be missed each time, so real programmes sit between the "same children" and "random" cases, not at the "different children" extreme. The operational goal is higher coverage and weaker correlation, not deliberately reaching different children, which would give up continuous protection through the peak.

Each SMC round (orange marker) starts a fresh protection curve that then decays, so cases fall furthest just after a round and recover in the gaps between rounds. The rounds prevent the most disease when their combined protection sits over the case peak. Move the peak away from the rounds, or widen the spacing, and the averted area shrinks.

What to notice

Protection is a scalloped envelope, not a flat shield. Because each dose decays, cases with SMC dip after every round and partly recover before the next one. Spacing the rounds about a month apart, chosen so protection has not fully waned before the next round, keeps those recoveries small; widening the spacing lets cases climb back between rounds.

Alignment with the peak matters more than the number of doses. A block of rounds centred on the case peak averts far more than the same rounds sitting on the shoulder of the season. Sweep the first-round slider with everything else fixed: the averted figure rises to a maximum when the rounds cover the peak and falls away on either side.

The penalty for mistiming grows with seasonality. Raise the seasonality slider so the peak is narrow, then shift the peak while leaving the rounds fixed. A concentrated season loses more when the calendar is off, because most of its cases sit in a short window that the rounds now miss. A flatter season is more forgiving.

Time to the cases, not the first rains. Transmission and cases lag the rainfall, so rounds set to the onset of the rains can lead the case peak and cover less than expected. Aligning to the observed peak in cases recovers the lost impact.3

Who is reached matters as much as how many. At a fixed coverage per round, switch between reaching the same, random and different children. Reaching the same children each round leaves the never-covered share fully exposed and spends later rounds on children who are often already protected, so it averts the least. Reaching random or different children draws more children in for at least one course (watch "children given ≥1 round" climb) and usually averts more. That gap narrows when coverage and the number of rounds are high, because then almost everyone is reached however the rounds overlap.

Methods. A deliberately simple, illustrative toy model showing the shape of how SMC impact depends on timing, not a specific setting; not to be used for decision making. Cases are shown in relative units on an arbitrary scale, so only the shape and the relative reductions are meaningful.

  • Seasonal cases. Childhood cases without SMC follow a periodic curve c0(t) = f + (1 − f)·s(t), where s(t) = ((1 + cos(2π(t − tpeak)/365))/2)k is a single yearly peak. The slider sets tpeak as the peak in cases; the toy does not simulate rainfall or the mosquito aquatic-stage lag (described in the concept sections), it places the case peak directly. The seasonality slider raises the peakiness k and lowers the off-season floor f together, from a near-flat perennial year to a sharp two-to-three month peak. The peak stays at the same height as seasonality changes, so the fixed vertical axis is comparable across settings; a consequence is that the total annual cases (the area) fall as seasonality rises, which is a display choice, not a claim that more seasonal places have less malaria, so the annual cases-averted percentage is best compared within a fixed seasonality. This is an illustrative stand-in for the seasonal clinical-incidence output of a full model in young children (the 3 to 59 month SMC age group); the immunity and prevalence detail is kept out of this page, as it lives in the other two explainers.
  • Protection per dose. One dose gives protection e(t) = exp(−(t/λ)k) against new infection at t days after the dose, the Weibull survival used for drug prophylaxis in malariasimulation,4 with the model's fitted SP-AQ parameters shape k = 4.3 and scale λ = 38.1 days. Panel 2 plots this curve directly.
  • An individual child's protection. Rounds are given at times ti (first round plus spacing times round number). A child's protection at time t comes from its most recent received dose, efficacy × maxi e(t − ti), taken over the rounds that child has already received, with the dose efficacy fixed at 0.9. In the code each covered child's protection is scaled by 0.9; this is equivalent, under the linear cases-to-protection reduction below, to 90% of doses fully protecting. Using the most recent dose (rather than adding doses) reflects that overlapping courses do not stack protection beyond a single effective course.
  • Coverage and who is reached. Coverage is the share of eligible children reached in each round. Population protection P(t) is the average of individual protection over children, which depends on how coverage is correlated between rounds. Same children: a fixed share equal to the coverage receives every round and the rest receive none. Random: each round reaches an independent draw, so the share receiving a given set of rounds follows independent per-round coverage. Different children: each round's covered group is rotated across children to minimise overlap, so the most children receive at least one course. All three hold the per-round coverage fixed and differ only in overlap between rounds.
  • Cases and readouts. Cases with SMC are c(t) = c0(t)·(1 − P(t)); the shaded area is c0(t)·P(t). Annual cases averted is that area as a share of all cases in the year. Peak-season cases averted uses only the months where c0 is at least half its maximum. Children given at least one round is the share reached by any round, which rises from the coverage (same children) towards reaching nearly everyone (random or different) when coverage and the number of rounds are high. The "rounds vs case peak" readout is the gap, in weeks, between the protection-weighted centre of the rounds and the case peak, with the sign showing early or late.
  • Direct protection only. Cases scale with each treated child's own protection, so the toy captures only the direct protection of children who receive SMC, and only its prophylactic (new-infection) effect. It omits the immediate clearance of existing infection and the transmission-reducing (indirect) effect of SMC, which in reality can also lower the peak for everyone. Both omissions mean the toy understates a well-timed programme's full impact.
  • Alignment with malariasimulation. The prophylaxis curve is taken directly from the package, so Panel 2 matches it exactly. Coverage correlation between rounds is a real model parameter, set with correlations$inter_round_rho("smc", rho): rho = 0 is the random case and rho = 1 is the same children. The package allows rho only in the range 0 to 1, so the "different children" (deliberately anti-correlated) option here is an idealised extension beyond it, included to show the far end of the trade-off. The seasonal case curve and the with-SMC reduction are qualitative: the cross-check in validation/validate_seasonality.R runs a highly seasonal profile with SMC in malariasimulation and confirms that childhood cases fall in a scalloped pattern tracking the rounds, that the case peak lags the rainfall peak, that cases averted fall when the rounds are shifted off the peak, and that at equal coverage random rounds (rho = 0) reach more children and avert more cases than the same children each round (rho = 1). The toy reproduces these shapes but not exact magnitudes.
