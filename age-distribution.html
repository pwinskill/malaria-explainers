The age distribution of malaria burden

Malaria is often called a disease of young children, but that is really only true where transmission is intense. As transmission rises, clinical cases pile up in the very young and fade in older children and adults; as it falls, the burden spreads back out across ages. The reason is immunity: the more infectious bites a person receives, the faster they build protection against clinical disease.1

1 Where transmission is intense, malaria is a disease of the very young

Evidence

Across African settings, the share of clinical malaria that falls in under-fives rises with transmission intensity: from below a fifth of cases where transmission is low to above three-fifths where it is high.1 Pooled analyses of age-stratified data show broadly consistent shifts, which also vary by clinical syndrome and season.2 The age at which severe disease and death occur also falls as transmission climbs, even though the total amount of severe disease does not simply rise with it.3

Mechanism

Exposure accumulates with age and with intensity. Where a child receives hundreds of infectious bites a year, they meet the parasite many times before school age; where they receive only a few, that same exposure is spread over decades. So the age by which a person has been infected often enough to be protected depends on how fast the bites arrive.

2 Repeated infection builds clinical immunity

Evidence

Immunity to malaria is acquired, not inborn, and it comes in stages. People in high-transmission areas keep getting infected throughout life, but with repeated exposure a smaller and smaller fraction of those infections turns into clinical illness.15 Clinical immunity does not stop infection; it stops infection from making you ill.

Mechanism

In the transmission model used here, the chance that a new infection becomes a clinical case falls as acquired immunity builds, following a saturating curve of cumulative exposure. Because the protection saturates, extra exposure matters most early: the first infections buy the biggest reductions in the chance of disease. Under intense transmission older children and adults have climbed high on that curve and are largely protected, so the clinical cases that remain are concentrated in the young, who have not yet caught up. (Exposure also builds a separate immunity that modestly reduces the chance a bite infects at all, but it is this clinical immunity that drives the shift in who gets ill.)

3 So the age of the burden tracks transmission

Evidence

When transmission falls, whether over years of control or between neighbouring areas, the burden that remains shifts into older children and adults, who now acquire immunity more slowly.4 The total number of cases can drop while the average age of a case rises.

Mechanism

The age distribution of cases is not fixed: it is a readout of transmission intensity. That has practical consequences. Interventions aimed at young children (such as seasonal chemoprevention, or the current vaccines delivered in early childhood) capture most of the burden where transmission is high, but a smaller share where it is low; and surveillance that only counts young children will increasingly miss cases as transmission falls.

What to notice

The young-child peak is made by immunity, not by biting. Infectious bites land on all ages (a little less on the very young, who are smaller). What concentrates clinical cases in young children at high transmission is that everyone older has already acquired protection.

Prevalence saturates; the age shift does not. Push the slider through the high end: parasite prevalence barely moves once it is high, but the under-5 share keeps climbing. Intensity keeps rewriting who gets ill even after it stops changing how many carry the parasite.

Falling transmission shifts the age burden to older age groups. Slide back down from intense to low and the spike flattens into a broad curve reaching into adulthood. Control that lowers transmission does not just shrink the burden, it shifts it to older age groups, which changes who interventions and surveillance should target. Each slider position is a long-run equilibrium, though: it shows where the age pattern settles, not the year-by-year path it takes to get there.

Methods. A deliberately simple, illustrative toy that runs the equilibrium solution of the Griffin model of Plasmodium falciparum transmission15 directly in your browser. Because it is an equilibrium solution, each slider position is the steady state a constant transmission intensity settles into; the toy does not capture the dynamics of a changing intensity, where immunity, and so the age distribution, would take years to catch up. It shows the shape of how the age distribution of disease depends on transmission intensity, not a specific place; not to be used for decision making.

  • The model. The page ports the human equilibrium solution from the malariaEquilibrium package (mrc-ide),6 using its standard fitted parameter set. For a given entomological inoculation rate (EIR, infectious bites per person per year) it solves, by age, the equilibrium of acquired immunity and the infection states (susceptible, diseased, asymptomatic, sub-patent). Biting heterogeneity between people is integrated out with a 10-point Gaussian quadrature, exactly as the package does.
  • Immunity and clinical disease. Three immunity functions build with cumulative exposure and age: pre-erythrocytic immunity (reducing the chance a bite infects), clinical immunity, and blood-stage/detection immunity. The probability that a new infection becomes a clinical case is φ = φ0(φ1 + (1−φ1)/(1 + ((IC)/IC0)κ)), a saturating (Hill) function of acquired clinical immunity IC; this term is what shifts disease to the young as exposure rises. Maternal immunity protects the first months of life.
  • Transmission intensity. The slider sets EIR on a log scale (roughly half a bite up to several hundred per person per year); the number itself is hidden because it is illustrative, and the parasite-prevalence readout gives the more familiar handle. Treatment is switched off (no antimalarial coverage) to keep the toy simple.
  • Age distribution and readouts. Clinical incidence is computed per age over 0–80 years and combined with the model's demographic age structure. The top chart shows the resulting distribution of cases across age, with each curve scaled to its own peak so only its shape is read. The bar sums that distribution into under-5, 5–15 and 15+ shares. Parasite prevalence is the model's microscopy-detectable prevalence in 2–10 year-olds; the two incidence readouts are clinical episodes per year for an under-5 child and averaged over all ages (the under-5 rate climbs far higher as transmission rises, while the all-age rate saturates). All figures are illustrative and shown on friendly scales (percentages and per-year rates), not raw model units.
  • Alignment with the model. Because the toy ports the equilibrium solver directly, it reproduces its outputs; the cross-check in validation/validate_age_distribution.R runs malariaEquilibrium across a range of EIRs and confirms the same age-group shares and prevalence, and that the under-5 share rises from well below a fifth at low transmission to above three-fifths at high transmission.
